Objective

The objective of chess is to checkmate your opponent's king. Checkmate occurs when the king is in a position to be captured (in check) and there is no legal move to escape the threat.

Setup

  • The chessboard is an 8x8 grid with alternating light and dark squares.
  • Each player starts with 16 pieces: 1 king, 1 queen, 2 rooks, 2 bishops, 2 knights, and 8 pawns.
  • The board is set up so that each player has a white square on their right-hand side.
  • Pawns are placed on the second row; rooks on the corners, knights next to rooks, bishops next to knights, the queen on her matching color, and the king on the remaining square.

Pieces and Movements

  • King: Moves one square in any direction.
  • Queen: Moves any number of squares in any direction (vertically, horizontally, or diagonally).
  • Rook: Moves any number of squares vertically or horizontally.
  • Bishop: Moves any number of squares diagonally.
  • Knight: Moves in an L-shape: two squares in one direction and then one square perpendicular. Knights can jump over other pieces.
  • Pawn: Moves forward one square but captures diagonally. On its first move, a pawn can move forward two squares.

Special Moves

  • Castling:
    • A move involving the king and either rook. The king moves two squares towards the rook, and the rook moves to the square next to the king. Conditions:
      • Neither the king nor the rook has moved before.
      • No pieces between the king and rook.
      • The king is not in check, does not move through check, or end in check.
  • En Passant:
    • If a pawn moves two squares forward from its starting position and lands beside an opponent's pawn, the opponent can capture it as if it moved only one square forward.
  • Pawn Promotion:
    • When a pawn reaches the opposite end of the board, it can be promoted to any other piece (except a king), typically a queen.

Gameplay

  • White moves first, and players alternate turns.
  • A player must move if they have a legal move available.
  • Pieces capture by moving into a square occupied by an opponent's piece.
  • Check: When a king is under threat of capture, it must be moved out of check, the threatening piece must be captured, or a piece must be placed in between.

Endgame and Scoring

  • Checkmate: The game ends when a player's king is checkmated.
  • Stalemate: The game is a draw if a player has no legal moves, and their king is not in check.
  • Draw: The game can also end in a draw by mutual agreement, insufficient material to checkmate, threefold repetition, or the fifty-move rule (no pawn move or capture in 50 consecutive moves).
  • Scoring: In tournaments, a win scores 1 point, a draw scores 0.5 points, and a loss scores 0 points.

Additional Rules

  • Touch-Move Rule: If a player touches a piece, they must move it if it has a legal move.
  • Illegal Moves: Making an illegal move can result in penalties or loss, depending on the rules of the tournament.
  • Time Controls: In timed games, players must make their moves within a set time. Failure to do so results in a loss.
  • Notation: Players often record their moves using algebraic notation, especially in tournaments.
